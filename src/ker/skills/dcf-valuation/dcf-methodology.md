# DCF Methodology Guide

## When to Use DCF

DCF analysis works best for:
- **Mature companies** with positive, relatively stable free cash flows
- **Companies with predictable growth** trajectories (consumer staples, utilities, industrials)
- **Evaluating acquisition targets** where intrinsic value matters more than market sentiment
- **Comparing intrinsic value to market price** to identify potential mispricing

## When NOT to Use DCF

DCF is unreliable or inappropriate for:
- **Pre-revenue / early-stage companies**: No cash flow history to project from. Use revenue multiples or comparable transactions instead.
- **Banks and financial institutions**: FCF is meaningless for banks — use dividend discount model or price-to-book.
- **REITs**: Use FFO (Funds From Operations) multiples, not DCF.
- **Highly cyclical businesses** (commodities, airlines): Normalized earnings or mid-cycle analysis is more appropriate.
- **Companies undergoing restructuring**: Historical cash flows are not representative of future state.
- **Biotech with binary pipeline outcomes**: Probability-weighted scenario analysis, not DCF.

## Interpreting the Sensitivity Matrix

The 3×3 sensitivity matrix varies WACC (±1%) and terminal growth (2.0%, 2.5%, 3.0%):

- **Range width indicates uncertainty**: A wide range ($80-$200) means the valuation is highly assumption-dependent — be cautious.
- **Cluster around current price**: If most scenarios cluster near market price, the stock is likely fairly valued.
- **All scenarios above current price**: Potential undervaluation, but verify the base assumptions aren't too optimistic.
- **All scenarios below current price**: Market may be pricing in growth that DCF doesn't capture, or the stock is overvalued.

## Common Pitfalls

1. **Over-optimistic growth rates**: Most companies cannot sustain >10% FCF growth for 5+ years. Always decay the growth rate.
2. **Terminal value dominance**: If terminal value is >80% of enterprise value, your near-term projections are too conservative relative to your long-term assumptions.
3. **Ignoring capital intensity**: High-growth companies often require heavy reinvestment that depresses FCF. Ensure CapEx assumptions are realistic.
4. **WACC sensitivity**: Small changes in WACC produce large valuation swings. Use the sensitivity matrix, not a single point estimate.
5. **Garbage in, garbage out**: The model is only as good as the inputs. Cross-validate growth rates with analyst estimates and historical performance.
6. **Anchoring to the result**: The DCF gives a range, not a precise number. Treat it as one input among many (multiples, comps, qualitative factors).
